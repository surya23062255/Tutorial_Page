Generations of Computer

Computers are classified between generations based on their developmental age. Different technologies were used to develop different types of computers. According to the type of 'processor' installed in a machine, there are five generations of computers.

First Generation (1945 - 1955) : Vacuum Tube

The first generation computers were very large in size. In the first generation of computers had been used Vacuum Tube technology which make computers possible to do calculations.
Example :- ENIAC and UNIVAC

Characterstics of First-Generation Computers

  • First-Generation Computers were based on Vacuum Tube technology.
  • They were very expensive to operate and used a huge amount of electricity.
  • First generation computers were the fastest computing devices of the time.
  • First generation computers lacked versatility and speed.
  • First generation computers were very large and required a lot of space for installation.

Second Generation (1955 - 1965) : Transistors

Second generation computers used Transistors in place of vacuum tubes. Transistors are made of semiconductor materials such as germanium and silicon. With the use of transistors computers became smaller, faster, cheaper and more reliable than their predecessors.
Example :- IBM-1401, IBM-7090

Characterstics of Second-Generation Computers

  • Second-Generation Computers were based on Transistor technology.
  • Second-Generation Computers were smaller as compared to First-Generation Computers.
  • Second generation computers were more portable and generated less amount of heat.
  • The computational time of second generation computers was reduced from milliseconds to microseconds.
  • Second generation computers were more reliable and less prone to hardware failure. Therefore they require less frequent maintenance.

Third Generation (1965 - 1971) : Integrated Circuit

Third generation computers were based on integrated circuit (IC) technology. The IC replaced many individually wired transistors. This development made computers smaller in size, reliable and efficient. The second generation computers were much smaller in size and were much faster than the previous generation computers. Instead of punched cards and printouts, the user interacted with third-generation computers and interfaced with the operating system via keyboard and monitor.
Example :- IBM-360 Series, TDC-316 etc.

Characterstics of Third-Generation Computers

  • Third-Generation Computers were based on Integrated Circuits (IC) technology.
  • Third-Generation Computers were more smaller compared to previous-generations Computers.
  • The computational time of third generation computers was reduced from microseconds to nanoseconds.
  • Hardware on third-generation computers rarely failed, so maintenance costs were also significantly lower.
  • Commercial production just got easier and cheaper

Fourth Generation (1971 - 1980) : Microprocessors

Fourth generation computers were developed by advances in IC technology of third generation computers. In the fourth generation, computers became smaller and cheaper than other generation computers. Fourth generation computers became more powerful, compact, reliable and affordable. As a result, it gave rise to the personal computer (PC) revolution.

Characterstics of Fourth-Generation Computers

  • Fourth-Generation Computers are based on Microprocessor technology.
  • Fourth-Generation Computers are very small in size.
  • The GUI and pointing devices enabled users to learn how to use the computer quickly.
  • Hardware failure of fourth-generation computers is nigligible so minimum maintenance is required.
  • The Production cost of fourth-generation computers is very low.

Fifth Generation (1980 - Till Date)

The dream of building a human-like computer that would be able to reason and reach a decision through a series of "what-if-then" analyses. These are the development objectives of the fifth generation of computers. The process of developing fifth generation computers is still in the development stage.

Characterstics of Fifth-Generation Computers

  • Mega Chips : Fifth generation computers will use Super Large Scale Integrated (SLSI) chips, resulting in the production of microprocessors that contain millions of electronics components on a single chip. Mega chips could enable computers to approximate the memory capacity of the human mind.
  • Parallel Processing : Computers with one processor access execute only one instruction at a time. This is called Serial Processing. However, fifth generation computers would use multiple processors and perform parallel processing, allowing multiple instructions to be accessed and worked on at the same time.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) : It refers to a range of related technologies that try to simulate and reproduce human behavior, including thinking, speaking, and reasoning. AI includes a set of related technologies: expert systems, natural language processing, speech recognition, vision recognition and robotics.
